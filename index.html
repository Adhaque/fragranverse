Fragrance glossary ✦

Fragrance Glossary

57 terms explained simply. No jargon, no fluff. Written for collectors in India and the Middle East who want to understand what they are buying.

Concentration
EDT
Eau de Toilette. Contains around 5 to 15 percent fragrance oil. Projects well for the first hour or two, then fades by hour four or five. Best for casual daytime use.
EDP
Eau de Parfum. Contains 15 to 20 percent oil. Lasts six to ten hours and sits closer to the skin. The most common format for Indian daily wear.
Parfum / Extrait
Contains 20 to 30 percent oil. Lasts eight to twelve hours or more. The projection is softer but the scent is the most intense. Best for evenings, dates, and air conditioned environments.
Attar
Traditional Indian and Middle Eastern perfume in oil form, with no alcohol. Applied directly to pulse points and lasts all day. Mysore rose attar and oud attars are community favourites. Very personal and does not project much into a room.
Performance
Top Notes
The first thing you smell after spraying. Usually citrus, bergamot, or light herbs. They disappear within ten to twenty minutes. Think of them as the opening hook of the fragrance.
Mid Notes
The heart of the fragrance. Florals, spices, and lavender usually live here. Lasts two to four hours. This is what the people around you smell most of the time.
Base Notes
The foundation. Woods, musk, vanilla, and amber stay six to twelve hours or more. They linger on clothes long after you have left the room.
Dry Down
The final stage of a perfume, usually three to four hours in, when the top and mid notes are gone and only the base remains. This is where a perfume reveals its true character.
Projection
How far the scent travels from your body. Strong projection means people can smell you across the room. Indian heat tends to amplify projection significantly.
Sillage
The scent trail you leave behind when you walk. Related to projection but not the same thing. Sillage is what lingers in the air after you have already left the room.
Longevity
How long you can smell the perfume on your skin. The community rates it as short (2 to 4 hours), moderate (4 to 6 hours), long lasting (6 to 8 hours), or eternal (8 to 12 hours and beyond).
AC Environment
Air conditioning dramatically reduces how far a perfume projects. A fragrance that fills a room outdoors may become a soft skin scent the moment you walk into an air conditioned office. Heavy orientals and ouds hold up better in AC. Light citrus and aquatic fragrances can almost disappear. If you wear perfume mostly indoors, choose something with strong base notes.
Humidity
High humidity amplifies everything. A fragrance that smells balanced in winter can become overwhelming in a Mumbai or Chennai summer. Heat and moisture push the top notes to project harder and faster, which means they also fade faster. In humid weather, lighter concentrations like EDT work better, and one or two sprays is usually enough.
Maceration
Letting a new bottle sit in a cool dark place for two to four weeks before judging it. Middle Eastern perfumes often need this time to settle. The smell deepens and performance improves. Common advice for Armaf, Afnan, and Lattafa bottles.
Reformulation
When a brand changes the formula of a perfume, usually to cut costs or meet safety regulations. According to Fragranverse data, over 170 niche perfumes launched before 2015 show signs of reformulation, with most changes happening after the EU tightened fragrance ingredient restrictions in 2012 and 2014. Oakmoss, nitro musks, and several citrus materials were restricted during this period. The number one complaint in the community is that a new batch does not smell the same as the original. Batch codes help you identify which version you have.
Batch
The production run a specific bottle comes from. Tells you roughly when it was made and which formula it uses. Pre August 2024 Hawas is a well known example of why batch awareness matters.
Scent Families
Citrus
Lemon, bergamot, grapefruit, orange. Fresh, sharp, and clean. Think nimbu paani in perfume form. A summer staple and a common top note in almost every men's fragrance.
Woody
Sandalwood, cedar, vetiver. Warm, dry, and grounded. The backbone of masculine perfumery and one of the most common families in our database.
Floral
Rose, jasmine, lily. Sweet, romantic, and classic. Modern florals appear in many unisex perfumes and are not just for women anymore.
Oriental
Rich, warm, and spicy. Vanilla, amber, resins, and incense. The masala chai of perfumery. Deep, complex, and long lasting. Very popular in India and the Middle East.
Oud
A scent family built around agarwood resin. Rich, woody, slightly animalic, and deeply complex. The defining family of Middle Eastern perfumery. What smells medicinal at first becomes addictive over time. One of the 14 canonical families tracked by Fragranverse.
Fresh
A modern category combining citrus, aquatic, and clean musk. Blue fragrances sit here. They smell like an expensive shower gel. Dylan Blue, Sauvage, and YSL Y are community favourites for all season wear.
Aquatic
Sea breeze, water, and marine notes. Cool, airy, and fresh. Perfect for gym wear and humid weather. Think Cool Water or Nautica Voyage.
Aromatic
Herbs and green notes. Lavender, rosemary, sage. Clean and traditionally masculine. Often paired with citrus or woody notes to add freshness.
Gourmand
Smells like dessert. Vanilla, caramel, chocolate, coffee. Sweet, warm, and very easy to like. Khamrah, Qahwa, and 9PM all lean into this family.
Spicy
Pepper, cinnamon, cardamom, saffron, and clove. Adds warmth, energy, and personality. Very popular in oriental and winter fragrances. Spicebomb Extreme is the benchmark reference in the community.
Leather
Birch tar and smoky suede. Dark, bold, and not for everyone. Found in Club de Nuit Intense Man, Ombre Leather, and Tuscan Leather.
Green
Fresh cut grass, leaves, stems. Crisp and natural. Not sweet, not woody, just green. Adds realism to floral or citrus compositions.
Chypre
Citrus on top, florals in the middle, mossy and woody at the base. Earthy, sophisticated, and timeless. Less common in budget Middle Eastern perfumes but well respected in niche houses.
Musk
Warm, clean, skin like. Modern white musk is nothing like old animal musk. Found at the base of almost every fresh fragrance on the market.
Community Terms
Clone
A budget perfume that copies the scent DNA of an expensive original. Turathi Blue is a good example. To a beginner it reads like Paco Rabanne Invictus. To an experienced nose it smells closer to Bvlgari Tygar. Same perfume, different reference depending on your exposure.
Clone Tier
Fragranverse classifies every perfume by tier. Budget is Tier 1, Value is Tier 2, Designer is Tier 3, and Niche is Tier 4. Recommendations respect your tier so a budget collector is never suggested a fragrance they cannot afford.
Flanker
A variation of an existing perfume. Hawas Ice is a flanker of the original Hawas. Same core DNA, different twist. Most popular fragrances have multiple flankers.
Beast Mode
Extremely strong projection and longevity. Fills a room and lasts ten hours or more. Hawas, Club de Nuit Intense Man, and Kaaf are frequently called beasts by the community.
Blind Buy
Buying a perfume without smelling it first, based only on reviews and note descriptions. The community rule of thumb is to try a decant first and only blind buy if the price is under 1,500 rupees.
Decant
A small sample of two to ten millilitres rebottled from a full bottle. Usually costs between 100 and 500 rupees. The number one piece of advice in the community before committing to a full bottle.
Dumb Reach
The perfume you grab without thinking. Easy, versatile, and reliable everywhere. Community examples include Turathi Blue, Dylan Blue, and YSL Y. Your daily no brainer.
Compliment Getter
A perfume that regularly makes strangers say you smell good. Tends to have strong projection and a mass appealing character. Club de Nuit Intense Man, Hawas, and 9PM are top compliment getters in the Indian community.
Skin Scent
A fragrance that stays very close to the body. Only you and someone standing very close can smell it. Some people prefer this for office settings or intimate occasions.
ME Fragrance
Middle Eastern perfume houses like Armaf, Afnan, Lattafa, Rasasi, and Al Haramain. Known for strong clones, excellent performance, and budget friendly prices. The backbone of the Indian collector community.
Accord
A blend of notes that creates a completely new smell together. Like how individual spices combine to make garam masala. Not one ingredient but the result of mixing several.
DNA
The core scent profile shared between related perfumes. Saying something has the same DNA means it shares the same fundamental character, even if the execution differs slightly.
Buyer Terms
Tester
A full perfume bottle sold without the retail box. Same fragrance inside, usually twenty to thirty percent cheaper. Popular among community members buying from WhatsApp sellers.
Vintage
An older batch of a perfume, often considered superior to current versions. Pre 2024 Hawas is a good example. Reformulations make vintage batches more sought after over time.
IFRA
International Fragrance Association. Sets global safety limits on fragrance ingredients. When your favourite perfume suddenly smells different, IFRA probably restricted an ingredient the brand relied on.
Atomizer
The spray mechanism on a perfume bottle. A good atomizer produces a fine mist with even coverage. Armaf bottles are well known in the community for having excellent atomizers.

Sources: Fragranverse database (7,527+ perfumes), community consensus and industry standard glossary
